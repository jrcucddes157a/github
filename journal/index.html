Visual Thinking Strategies

2/10/2026

We often hear, are taught, and use critical thinking skills in our daily lives, but it tends to be the norm that we don't think of the possibilities of its use case in anything other than writing applications. For example, critical thinking is often used in literary analysis for English classes, or when making a point in an argumentative essay. Applying it visually, however, is an entirely different case and one that I think should often get more attention than in just art history classes or topics. Especially since requiring people to understand what is going on through merely their eyes and process properly what's happening is becoming more and more important. I think learning about these skills and gaining is super important.

The New York Times offers this in their Photo of the Week every Monday, where they present a picture without context or captions and ask students around the country to participate and figure out what is going on or what the picture is telling them. In the article, they outline strategies for creating deeper visual engagement, and I think they are all quite effective.

Though the most interesting thing left out is how all of these photos deliberately engage users to interact with it based on the absurdity of the imagery, or genuinely attract audiences. For example, the first image is already an eye-catcher because of the striking image of a young boy diving from one trahs pile to another. It's through this striking imagery that people want to engage further. But it's not just images that can create this, but websites too.

In researching the application for the website design side and how the strategies apply, I look to the awesome website created by Ray Ban as a promotional campaign, Ray-Ban.EXE. To create visually interesting images, it takes on a Korean inspired cyberpunk visual that not only imitates the aesthetic down to striking colors, retrofuture visuals that emulate CRTs, but also affects the interactivity. For example, when moving the cursor, the image of a character moves in a 3D space in direct opposite reaction. As well, the cursor has been changed to something more sci-fi. There's even sound you can turn on and off that makes the interaction much more tactile and noticeable.

To me, this is an insanely cool website and already successfully creates the first step. It has me wondering what exactly the story behind such an ad-campaign is, and shows in the upper right corner the viewable anime that perhaps explains everything.

Research on Overlay Design Patterns

1/29/2026

I found the article Overuse of Overlays: How to Avoid Miusing Lightboxes both a helpful piece of information, but also an endlessly fascinating piece of a time capsule for multiple reasons.

When it comes to the information presented, even if by current standards of web design a decade after it is published, I feel like some of the principles still apply to this day. For example, one of the listed "good reasons" to use an overlay stated by author Kathryn Whitenton is that it requests users for critical information required to proceed. The cited example she uses is Chase Bank's website asking for a ZIP code on the page involving home mortgage quotes, as location is a huge dependency for the rates. Many sites still use that currently.

For example, go on Google right now on a "incognito tab" and search up something like "Chinese restaurants". An overlay appears on the screen where Google will ask you to share precise location data to get the most accurate and relevant search results to your location. There you have two clear cut options to exit the overlay. Either "Not now" as your cancellation option, or "Use precise location" with a crosshair for confirmation.

The antiquity and amusement however comes from seeing all the photos of 2015 era websites both on mobile devices and a desktop/laptop Mac screenshot. You'll find all kinds of design features we don't use anymore on sites like shading, gradients, noticeable drop shadows, borders, etc. Even some of the buttons like in the mentioned Chase example have that exact "Frutiger Aero" 3D-esque aesthetic to their buttons that was reflected in operating systems at the time, Windows 7/8, and Mac OS X Mavericks/Yosemite. You could only just barely see aspects of the blooming modern flat style appearing on sites like Apple's website. That's what makes it such a fascinating time capsule to me.

Best Practices for Form Design

01/18/2026

Doing research for best practices on form design, I read the following article linked below. Reading it, I agreed with many things and thought the article provided useful information. Even if some of the information I felt, as a designer, was extremely obvious. For example, making sure that the length of certain form inputs matching the length of the content. It's expected and often intuitive that the box should be shorter when inputting information like ZIP codes. As well, for the sake of mobile sites and preventing incorrect inputs, we force the keyboard to only display numbers or the full alphanumeric keyboard. A lot of it is standard fare.

I only found myself raising an eyebrow at a part in suggestion number 16 when talking about providing a "show password" option. The author quotes Jakob Nielsen, and he says:

"Typically, masking passwords doesn’t even increase security, but it does cost you business due to login failures."

Good lord, NO! On what planet is it not secure to mask your password? You have no idea who's looking over your shoulder at your screen. Especially in a day and age when display technology, especially on portable devices, really values high brightness and clarity for ease of readability for smartphone users. It is a good option to include a "Show Password" option in case of typing mistakes, sure. I use it myself sometimes, but this implication is dangerous and I think regardless all passwords should always be masked. Besides, consider it typing practice!
